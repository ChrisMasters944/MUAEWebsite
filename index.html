Analyzing the striking design choices of the popular survival game, to understand how it evokes a sense of wonder in its players.

What is Raft?

Raft is a survival game where you are stranded in the middle of the vast ocean, on nothing but a single piece of wood. You pick up pieces of trash in the ocean to add to your raft, and it is your job to survive, all while dangerous animals hunt you. You need to eat, drink water, and guide your raft in hopes of finding land.

What's at stake?

In this game, your very survival is always at stake. It is up to you to make the delicate balance between expanding your raft, drinking water, gathering food, and fighting off sharks. This game is not for the faint of heart, and by neglecting any of these tasks, you could wind up dead.

Narrative arc

Your story starts as a simple fight for survival, but as you progress in the game you make discoveries, such as islands with rich wildlife, radio towers, and abandoned wreckage. You eventually learn that human civilization was destroyed by rising sea levels. The story is told through journals, notes, and environmental storytelling, hinting at scientific experiments, political conflicts, and the desperate struggle for survival by the few remaining humans.

Ending

Like many popular survival games, there is no true "ending" to Raft. However, there is a final part to the story of the game, where the player explores the floating utopia of Tangaroa, a city created by humans after the rise of sea levels in an attempt to survive. You quickly learn how flawed the city is, and you have the choice between returning to your raft to continue to survive, or try to rebuild civilization at Tangaroa.

Deeper meaning

Raft throws you into the deep end when you start the game, every next sunrise you see being a gift. However as you progress through the game, each victory or problem solved gives you the confidence and satisfaction to keep going, and the whole game is a continuous climb towards that feeling of completeness. This game masterfully plays on your desire to progress and improve, without revealing its hand, keeping the magic alive.

Visual Design

The visual design of raft is crucial to understand when discussing the game's immersive nature. The graphics straddle the line between cartoonish and semi-realistic, which is important to the unassuming behavior of the visual aesthetic. By not drawing attention to itself, the textures step aside and allow other aspects of the game to lure you in.

The game employs a vibrant color scheme to highlight the beauty of the world you are in. The deep blue of the ocean contrasts perfectly against the warm tones of brown on your raft, and the exotic colors of the greenery and wildlife. Along with the deep reflection of the moon on the water, and the wild colors of the sunset, these elements serve to give players a sense of attachment and fondness to the world they are trying to survive in. This beauty gives players at the more reason to survive and fight.

Soundscape

Soundtrack
Raft's soundtrack was composed by Jannik Schmidt, and it is a hollistic addition to game. The full OST runs just over 24 minutes, and the music itself is comprised of natural elements such as piano, strings, and tribal drums. The minimalistic harmonic profile and clever orchestration in the music help immerse the player in the somewhat tropical and ocean aesthetic of the game, and the tracks all inspire hope in the player.
Water
Being that you are stranded at sea pretty much the whole game, it should come as no surpise that water and its soundscape are a vital aspect to the game. Ambience
An important aspect of game design where Raft is concerned is sound design. The developers cleverly crafted all the UI sounds to be high frequency and unassuming. By staying subtle with its ambient sounds, the immersion isn't broken when you interact with the UI.
